**[06-paws-and-reflect-072]** · page 48 · 127w · _ashamed, guilty, discouraged, hopeful_

**[06-paws-and-reflect-073]** · page 48 · 117w · _ashamed, guilty, forgiven, hopeful_

The key difference between guilt and shame is this: guilt says, “I did something bad,” while shame says, “I 48 am bad.” Guilt can lead to positive change. We don’t have to earn it, work for it, or prove we deserve it. We just have to honestly admit what we did wrong and receive what God freely offers.

**[06-paws-and-reflect-074]** · page 49 · 94w · _ashamed, guilty, forgiven, striving_

But forgiveness doesn’t stop with God. Jesus taught us to pray, “Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors” (Matthew 6:12). Part of receiving forgiveness is learning to forgive others and to forgive ourselves. This is particularly challenging for teenagers, who often struggle with perfectionism and self-criticism. We might find it easier to forgive friends for their mistakes than to forgive ourselves for ours. But 49 holding onto guilt after God has forgiven us isn’t humility; it’s pride. It’s as if we are saying that our standards are higher than God’s.

---

**[06-paws-and-reflect-075]** · page 50 · 102w · _ashamed, guilty, forgiven, restored_

Admit the damage honestly. Don’t minimize, excuse, or blame others. Take responsibility for our choices and their consequences. Make things right where possible. Apologize sincerely. Replace what can be replaced. Learn what needs to be learned. Accept forgiveness gracefully. Whether it comes from God, others, or ourselves, receive it as the gift it is. Don’t keep punishing ourselves after we’ve already made it right. Learn and move forward. Let the experience teach you something valuable, but don’t let it define you. Extend the same grace to others. When friends mess up, remember how good it felt to be forgiven instead of criticized.

**[06-paws-and-reflect-076]** · page 50 · 98w · _ashamed, guilty, forgiven, hopeful_

### Lesson 11: The Patient Therapy Dog

**[06-paws-and-reflect-080]** · page 53 · 94w · _called, striving, loved_

Daisy shows us something beautiful about true service: it’s not about us. It’s not about feeling good about ourselves, getting praise, or even seeing results right away. It’s about showing up every time and offering whatever others need, whether that’s energy, calm, entertainment, or simply being there. For teenagers, this can be an unusual idea. We’re often encouraged to serve in ways that look good on college applications or make us feel important. But Daisy reminds us that the most meaningful service often happens quietly, without fanfare, in response to real needs around us.

**[06-paws-and-reflect-081]** · page 53 · 124w · _called, grateful, trusting_

Real service requires some of Daisy’s key qualities: patience (things don’t always go as planned), flexibility (different people need different things), and 53 persistence (showing up even when you don’t feel like it). It also requires genuine care for others rather than doing it for ourselves. “Each of you should use whatever gift you have to serve others, as faithful stewards of God’s grace in its various forms.” — 1 Peter 4:10 We have gifts to offer, not someday when we’re older and more qualified, but right now. Serving others and being a good steward is connected. The abilities, opportunities, and resources we have aren’t just for our own benefit. They’re tools God has blessed us with so that we can also bless others.

---

**[06-paws-and-reflect-082]** · page 54 · 138w · _called, loved, joyful_

Jesus is a perfect example. Even though He was literally God, He spent His time washing dirty feet, healing sick people, feeding hungry crowds, and listening to individuals who needed help. In Mark 10:43, He said: “Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant.” This doesn’t mean we have to become a therapy dog. It means looking for ways to serve that match our gifts 54 and the needs around us. Good with kids? Help with Sunday school or babysit for free for a struggling family. Tech savvy? Teach older adults how to use smartphones or help teachers with computer problems. A good listener? Simply be available when friends need to talk through problems. Organized? Help coordinate service projects or assist with community events. Athletic? Coach younger kids or organize activities for people with disabilities.

**[06-paws-and-reflect-083]** · page 55 · 93w · _called, discouraged, trusting_

Start where we are, with what we have, serving people we can actually reach. Daisy doesn’t try to help every sick child in the world; she faithfully serves the ones in her hospital. We don’t have to solve global problems to make a real difference. Also, like Daisy, be prepared for service to sometimes be harder than expected. People might not appreciate our efforts. Our help might not solve their problems completely or even a little bit. We might not see results right away, and that’s okay. Faithful service is about unchanging love.

---

**[06-paws-and-reflect-084]** · page 55 · 95w · _called, proud, joyful_

Look for ways to serve that’s not about us at all. Don’t post about it on social media. Don’t expect thank-you notes. Don’t count it toward service hours. Just quietly help someone who needs it, the way Daisy helps her young patients. 55 We might discover that serving others is actually one of the most fulfilling things we can do. Not because it makes us look good, but because it connects us to our purpose and lets us be part of God’s work of healing the world, one small act of kindness at a time.
